Scaffolded knowledge integration framework (SKIF) is an educational design resulting from the synthesis of curricula programs (i.e., Computer as Learning Partner) developed for thermodynamics (Linn, 2000). In SKIF, students use the methods to link, connect, and integrate their understandings about a domain into a cohesive basis. From this unified base, students can apply their understandings to problems that are meaningful to them. The drive for SKIF is enabling students to examine new knowledge from their own perspective and conceptual framework, rather than adopt a view from the instructor (Linn, 2000). From this student-centric level of progression, SKIF sees student knowledge as building blocks and encourages students to resolve discrepancies over memorizing facts and events.

Students enter into novel learning environments with some foundation to think from. Students have complex knowledge about models (e.g., heuristics, algorithms, representations, mechanisms, etc.) and need to consider how to expand, compare, ameliorate, define, integrate, and connect new information into their existing perspectives (Linn et al., 1994, Linn, 2000). In order to help students, whom are novices in a subject matter, SKIF is designed to facilitate the process where students improve upon the sophistication of knowledge models as well as distinguish among them.

The intent of this framework is not necessarily about the progression from novice to expert, rather it is about providing students with a strong foundation for future learning. Students will have a wide repertoire of models, rather than focusing on a single “correct” model or replacing models. This attitude toward learning can help prepare students to act as informed citizens (Linn, 2000).

Beginning with existing ideas, students can build and integrate new knowledge that connects to what they already know. The accessibility can be met through scaffolding that builds up student understandings, or may bring in students' outside-of-school interests. An undertone of this framework is to connect the new models and knowledge to everyday life. In this perspective, what should be learned is how we use science in daily life rather than generate a new kind “school only science.” By making this connection to the personal, the content to be learned switches from being driven by expert and abstract framing, which encourages memorization and isolation of new information, to being a meaningful experience that registers with existing ideas (Linn & Hsi, 2000).

However, this does not mean that the learning should be something that is easy or comfortable to the student. Rather, the goal should be to base the learning on student knowledge, but then to provide an engaging amount of difficulty so the student can develop new models and understanding to explain the discrepancy between what they know and the new knowledge.

Students have many models of knowledge, and as they negotiate the differences between their existing knowledge and the new content, the need ways to evaluate the evidence of their thinking. The balance between becoming aware of one's thinking and having the teacher (or content) direct responsibility is delicate. Too little scaffolding or missing skills may hinder knowledge integration as a student struggles to exercise self-learning (e.g., Duschl, 1990; Raizen, 1991), but by making thinking too visible and explicit encourages memorization over understanding (Linn, 2000).

The intent of making thinking visible is to have reflection and activities that initiate an understanding of what has been done—exploring the hows and whys. Such skills can be developed through acting as investigators to critique and evaluate evidence, reflecting on alternative explanations and situations, reflecting on how ideas change and develop, modeling dynamic systems, comparing expert-to-novice pathways, and engaging in continuous reasoning for develop an area expertise (Linn, 2000).

Learning, philosophers and educations have asserted, is a social medium ( e.g., Dewey, 1901; Dewey, 1929). Whether from Vygotsky's (1978) “zone of proximal development” or from communities of learners (Scardamalia & Bereiter, 1993), groups of students can leverage distributed knowledge to overcome challenges and support fellow learning. This can result in novice students learning expert behavior from more skilled students—communities of practice (Brown & Campione, 1990; Lave & Wenger, 1991; Newman et al., 1989; Pea, 1992). The balance for social inclusion is delicate as well.

Groups sizes of 3-4 can initiate exclusion due to limited workspace, gender can develop into power dynamics, leaders can gain the most experience, and work can be distributed rather than collaborative, meaning that students work alone and combine results (Burbules & Linn, 1991; Madhok, 1992 April 4 & 5). In order for peer support to be most effective, the activity needs to help students build and integrate knowledge together. Smaller groups can help when designing activities so that the work students do relies on joint action in learning (Linn, 1992b).

How students learn in school shouldn't be an exception from learning in life. Students should resonate with what they are learning about, as well as how they are learning the content. The approaches used during learning and the repertoire of models can designed to be reusable and have utility in life. The common properties of inquiry (e.g., critiquing perspectives, planning experiments, testing ideas, and evaluating models) can give a student a strong general ability at inquiry (Linn, 2004).

The development and recognition of these life-long utility skills leads students being autonomous learners—they become natural learners. While going through a SKIF experience, students should be encouraged to find and reflect on their knowledge models a nd evaluate their success in overcoming challenges. Other things like receiving feedback, writing summaries, making outlines, generating questions, and reflecting can help increase the quality of the learned knowledge (Linn, 2004). Revisiting knowledge (either simply, or from a more challenging perspective) can also help mimic life-long learning, with the additional benefit that retrieving information impacts knowledge utility more than traditionally studying the same content (Bjork, 1994).

Apart from operational and in-line strategies, a final design can be to rely on sustained reasoning (i.e., one central topic to a high degree). With a central area of focus, students can develop a powerful base of information that helps them bridge to new ideas. Having deep experience in one domain lets students begin to organize knowledge and develop nuances of connected situations—making a solid base for future learning (Linn, 2004).
